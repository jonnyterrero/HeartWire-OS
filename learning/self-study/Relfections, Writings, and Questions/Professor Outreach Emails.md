## How to Use This Document

1. Pick the template that matches your ask (research, guidance, or informational meeting).
2. Fill in every `[BRACKET]` with professor-specific details.

## Field-Specific Customization Notes

### Biomedical Engineering Labs
- Lead with engineering skills: signal processing, instrumentation, embedded systems, biomechanics simulations.
- Mention specific tools if relevant: LabVIEW, MATLAB, Arduino, FEA software.
- If you've built anything — even a prototype — mention it. BME labs value builders.
- Example project hook: *"I've been building a low-cost ECG front-end circuit using an INA128 and Arduino, which connected your work on wearable biosensing directly for me."*

### Neuroscience / Neural Engineering Labs
- Emphasize interest in the intersection of biology and engineering — this is what most neuro labs care about.
- Mention any signal processing work or interest in neural data (EEG, spike sorting, BCI).
- Self-studying neuroscience is worth mentioning — it signals initiative.
- Example hook: *"I've been self-studying neural engineering, focusing on BCI architectures and signal classification, which is what drew me to your lab's work on [their specific topic]."*

### Computational / AI/ML / Bioinformatics Labs
- Lead with technical skills: Python, relevant libraries (NumPy, PyTorch, scikit-learn), any ML projects.
- Mention GitHub if your repos are clean and relevant.
- If you've built a model, run a simulation, or contributed to a coding project — say it.
- Example hook: *"I've been implementing molecular dynamics simulations and studying computational chemistry methods, and your lab's application of ML to [topic] is exactly the direction I want to move."*

### Chemistry / Biochemistry / Physical Chemistry Labs
- Mention relevant coursework directly: Orgo I/II, Physical Chemistry, Analytical Chemistry.
- If you have any wet lab experience or self-study in computational chemistry, lead with that.
- These professors often value intellectual curiosity and theoretical grounding — show you've read their papers, not just their abstracts.
- Example hook: *"I've been self-studying quantum chemistry foundations alongside my Physical Chemistry coursework, and your group's work on [specific topic] is something I've been following closely."*

## Key Rules for All Emails

1. **Name something specific** in their work. One paper title, one project, one method. Generic emails get deleted.
2. **Lead with what you bring**, not what you want. Flip the frame from "I want experience" to "here's what I can contribute."
3. **Keep it under 200 words.** Everything after that loses them.
4. **One ask per email.** Don't ask for a lab spot AND a meeting AND guidance. Pick one.
5. **No flattery.** Skip "I find your work incredibly inspiring." Just say what you connected with and why.
6. **Send on Tuesday–Thursday morning.** Highest open rates in academia.
7. **Follow up once** if no response in 10–14 days. One sentence: *"I wanted to follow up on my previous email — I'm still very interested if you have a moment."*
