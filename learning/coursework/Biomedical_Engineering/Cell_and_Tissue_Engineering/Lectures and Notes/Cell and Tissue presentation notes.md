FTIR (Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy) — Key Notes
1. What is FTIR & Why It’s Used
Purpose
- Identifies chemical bonds and molecular structures via infrared (IR) absorption.
- Each bond vibrates at a unique frequency, creating a molecular fingerprint
Why it matters (biomedical relevance)
- Protein adsorption: detects protein denaturation on implants.
- Polymer degradation: monitors chemical breakdown of biodegradable polymers.
- Safety: checks for toxic residual monomers in medical-grade materials
2. FTIR Process & Components
Energy source
- Mid-infrared (IR) light  
Main components
1. IR source – emits IR radiation
2. Interferometer – beam splitter creates an interferogram (contains all frequencies)
3. Sample area – IR interacts with the material
4. Detector – measures transmitted or reflected IR
5. Fourier transform – converts raw signal into readable spectrum
6. FTIR Procedure & Data
Common method
- ATR-FTIR (Attenuated Total Reflection)  
- Sample pressed against high-refractive-index crystal (diamond or ZnSe
Output
- Spectrum of absorbance or transmittance vs. wavenumber (cm⁻¹)
Sample prep
- Minimal; works for solids, liquids, thin films
- Samples should be dry (water absorbs IR strongly)
Limitations
- Cannot analyze bulk metals (reflect IR)
- Water can mask important peaks
- Limited depth of penetration (surface-sensitive)
4. FTIR: Characterization vs. Modification
Characterization (Providing information)
- Identifies functional groups and chemical purity
- Mostly used for polymers, organic molecules, biological tissues
- Purpose: confirm material identity or detect contamination

Modification (Tailoring properties)
- Verifies surface chemical changes
- Used for surface-modified metals, ceramics, polymers
- Confirms grafting, coatings, or chemical bonding occurred
5. FTIR for Modification Monitoring
How it’s used
- Compare “before” vs. “after” spectra
Successful modification indicators
- New peaks → new functional groups
- Shifted peaks → new chemical bonds
Key requirement
- Samples must be dry to avoid false peaks from moisture
6. Surface Grafting (Definition & Purpose)
Definition
- Surface grafting bonds new chemical species to the outermost layer of a material
- Modifies surface properties without changing bulk properties
Example
- PEG-grafted surfaces prevent protein and cell adhesion (used in biosensors)
7. Grafting Techniques
Primary techniques
- Plasma activation  
- Creates surface radicals
- Used for polymers without functional groups
- Silane chemistry  
- Replaces surface –OH groups with silanes
- Used for metals and oxide surfaces
- EDC/NHS chemistry  
- Activates carboxyl & amine groups
- Used for polymers and hydrogels

Key steps

1. Activate surface (plasma or chemical)
2. Attach initiator or silane
3. Immerse in monomer + catalyst
4. Grow surface layer
5. Stop polymerization at desired thickness
6. Characterization Methods (Beyond FTIR)

Surface analysis

- XPS – elemental composition & chemical bonding
- FTIR – functional groups
- SEM – surface morphology
- AFM – nanoscale roughness

Quantitative measurements

- Grafting density
- Contact angle (hydrophilicity)
- Surface roughness
-
Biological testing

- Cell adhesion & proliferation
- Protein adsorption (fibronectin, albumin)
- DNA/protein quantification

Mechanical & physical testing

- Adhesion strength (shear, tensile)
- Durability & wear

Quality control

- Compare to ungrafted controls
- Assess stability, aging, degradation

9. Biomedical Applications

- Vascular grafts (PTFE, Dacron, polyurethanes)
- Drug-eluting stents
- Wound dressings
- Retinal implants
- Cell-surface engineered polymers (e.g., PLL-g-PEG)

10. Coating Outcomes (PVD vs. CVD)

Common results
- Very thin coatings (nm–µm)
- Improved surface chemistry, wettability, biocompatibility
- Bulk properties unchanged
-
Differences

PVD
- Thinner coatings
- High purity
- Not ideal for complex shapes
- Good for heat-sensitive substrates
-
CVD

- Excellent uniformity
- Good for complex geometries
- Requires very high temperatures

Surface Engineering & Coatings — Presentation Notes
Why Tailor Surface Chemistry?
- Surface chemistry = atoms, oxides, functional groups, and surface charge.
- Controls:  
- Which proteins adsorb
- Adsorption strength
- Whether proteins denature
- Key principle:  
	Cells do not attach directly to materials → they attach to the protein layer that forms first.
How it’s done
- PVD (Physical Vapor Deposition): deposits a thin film on top of the surface.
- CVD (Chemical Vapor Deposition): gas precursors chemically react to form surface films.
Surface Properties Tuned by PVD & CVD
1. Surface chemistry
2. Hydrophilicity / hydrophobicity
3. Surface roughness
4. Biocompatibility
5. Functional coatings
Surface Hydrophilicity / Hydrophobicity
- Affects protein adsorption behavior
- Proteins adsorb differently on:  
- Hydrophobic vs. hydrophilic surfaces
- Since cells bind to ECM proteins, changing wettability:  
- Alters cell adhesion
- Alters cell spreading and attachment
Surface Roughness
- Physical texture at micro- and nano-scale
- Influences:  
- Cell migration
- Cell proliferation
- Cell differentiation
- Nano-topography affects protein adsorption, which indirectly controls cell behavior
Biocompatibility
- Describes how the body responds at the material interface
- Governed by:  
- Protein layer formation
- Immune system interaction
- PVD/CVD can:  
- Add barrier coatings to reduce corrosion or ion release
- Improve osseointegration
- Promote bone mineralization
- Enable direct chemical bonding between implant and bone
Functional Coatings
- Thin films that give surfaces new functionality
- Common functions:  
- Bioactive
- Antimicrobial
- Barrier layers
- Wear resistance
Grafted Molecules (Surface Functionalization)
Categories & Examples
- Anticoagulants: Heparin
- Cell adhesion molecules: Collagen IV, Laminin
- Antifouling polymers:  
- PEG (polyethylene glycol)
- PNIPAM
- PEGMA
- Antimicrobial agents: 4-vinylpyridine
- Anti-inflammatory: CD47 proteins
- Biocompatibility enhancers:  
- Methacrylic acid (MAA)
- PLL-g-PEG copolymers
Combination approaches
- Multiple molecules grafted sequentially or simultaneously
- Example:  
- ECM proteins (collagen, laminin) first
- Followed by bioactive molecules (heparin, PGE1)
- Goal: dual or multifunctional surfaces (e.g., anticoagulant + cell adhesion)
Performance Outcomes & Advantages
- High grafting efficiency and uniform surface coverage
- Improved biological performance:  
- Increased cell adhesion
- Reduced nonspecific protein adsorption
- Lower inflammatory response
- Quantitative improvements:  
- 2–5× higher cell adhesion
- Increased proliferation
- Reduced contact angle (↑ hydrophilicity)
- Durability:  
- Covalently grafted layers outperform physically adsorbed coatings
- Better long-term stability and reproducibility
What Is Thermal Spraying?
- Coating process that heats or melts materials and deposits them onto a surface
- Applicable to:  
- Metals
- Ceramics
- Polymers
- Composites
- Used in biomedical implants  
- Example: hydroxyapatite plasma spray on hip/knee implants
- Enhances bone integration and patient outcomes
Air Plasma Spray (APS
- Uses high-pressure gas + electric arc
- Generates plasma ~15,000 °C
- Powder feedstock melts and deposits onto surface
- Very versatile:  
- Can coat nearly any material
- Differences vs HVOF:  
- Higher heat
- Slower deposition
- Useful for insulating materials
High Velocity Oxygen Fuel (HVOF)
- Uses high-velocity oxygen combustion
- Particle speeds > 1000 m/s (Mach 3)
- Advantages:  
- Extremely strong mechanical bonding
- Dense, corrosion-resistant coatings
- Typical thickness: ~0.1–2 mm
- Lower oxidation than other spray methods
- Limitation: lower operating temperature limits materials used
Coating & Substrate Interaction
- Heated particles impact surface and flatten into splats
- Adhesion mechanism:  
- Primarily mechanical interlocking (“hooking”)
- Coating selection depends on:  
- Service environment
- Desired mechanical, chemical, and biological properties

Mechanical Surface Modification, PVD & Characterization — Clean Notes
Effects & Results of Mechanical Surface Treatments
Purpose: Improve adhesion, fatigue life, and bonding by altering surface topography and stress state.
Key Methods → Results
- Sandblasting  
- Cleans surface
- Increases roughness
- → Better coating or adhesive adhesion
- Shot Blasting  
- Induces compressive residual stresses
- → Improves fatigue life
- Grit Blasting  
- Creates sharp surface texture
- → Strong mechanical interlocking/bonding
- Grinding  
- Produces controlled, uniform roughness
- → Consistent bond performance

Overall Mechanical Treatment Procedure

1. Surface preparation  
- Remove oils, debris, corrosion
1. Selection of abrasive media  
- Sand, grit, shot, abrasive paper, cloth
1. Abrasive velocity delivery  
- Control pressure, speed, nozzle distance
1. Surface modification  
- Roughening, stress induction, cleaning
1. Post-blasting cleaning  
- Remove loose particles and residues

Mechanical Treatment Categories
- Abrasive treatment
- Shot peening
- Wire brushing
- Grinding
- Milling
Abrasive Blasting — What & Why
Definition
- A surface texturization method where abrasive particles are fired at ~100 m/s.
Abrasive media examples
- Sand
- Aluminum oxide
- Diamond compounds
- Baking soda
Used for
- Cleaning
- Surface preparation
- Shaping or texturing prior to coating or bonding
Overall Procedure of Physical Vapor Deposition (PVD)
Purpose: Apply thin, dense, protective coatings without affecting bulk material.
Steps
1. Physical cleaning  

- Substrate must be extremely clean
- Heating / pumping  
- Degassing
- Achieve vacuum
1. Etching phase  
- Ion bombardment cleans surface at atomic level
1. Coating phase  
- Metal vapor travels through vacuum
- Condenses on substrate
- Techniques: sputtering, evaporation
1. Result  
- Hard, dense metallic or ceramic coating
- Thickness: much thinner than a human hair

HPLC — WHAT / WHY
What it does
- Separates and analyzes chemical compounds in liquid samples
- Outputs signal vs. time chromatograms
- Provides qualitative + quantitative data
Sample requirements
- Must be liquid or soluble
- Filtered (0.2–0.45 µm)
- Small volumes (µL scale)
Applications
- Pharmaceuticals
- Biological samples
- Food & beverages
- Cosmetics
- Industrial chemicals
Purpose
- Measure purity, impurities, degradation products
- Verify dose accuracy & release kinetics
- Support QC and regulatory approval
⚠️ HPLC does NOT modify materials — it characterizes chemical composition.
Key Takeaway (Exam-Level)
- Mechanical treatments & PVD → surface modification
- Abrasive blasting → roughness, adhesion, fatigue improvement
- PVD → thin, hard, protective coatings
- HPLC → chemical analysis, not surface engineering

Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)
WHAT / WHY
Basic Principle
- A high-energy electron beam is transmitted through an ultrathin specimen
- Image contrast arises from electron scattering within the sample
- Enables nanometer to sub-nanometer resolution
Why TEM matters
- Reveals internal structures not visible with optical microscopy
- Allows correlation between internal microstructure and material/biological function

TEM Characterization — Information Obtained

- Internal ultrastructure
- Crystallinity and phase information
- Nanoparticle size and distribution

Applicable to

- Materials
- Biological samples
- Polymers and composites
- Ceramics and nanoparticles
Why gather this data
- Understand structure–function relationships
- Evaluate nanoparticle uptake
- Analyze scaffold architecture
TEM Procedure & Output
- Requires ultrathin sections
- Operates under vacuum
- Produces:  
- High-resolution images
- Diffraction patterns (crystallographic info)

TEM Limitations

- Destructive sample preparation
- Not suitable for living samples
- Beam damage possible
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
WHAT IS SEM?
- Uses a focused beam of high-energy electrons to scan a surface
- Operates in vacuum
- Image formed from emitted secondary electrons
SEM Characterization — Information Obtained
- Surface morphology
- Surface topography
- Pore size and pore distribution
- Fiber alignment
Materials SEM can be used on
- Metals (e.g., titanium, stainless steel)
- Ceramics (e.g., hydroxyapatite, bioactive glass)
- Polymers (e.g., polyethylene, PTFE)
- Composites (fiber-reinforced polymers, carbon fiber)
Why SEM is Useful
- Detailed visualization of surface morphology
- Resolves micro- to nanoscale features
- Much higher resolution and depth of field than optical Useful

SEM Pros / Cons
Pros
- High-resolution greyscale images
- Excellent depth of field
- Detailed surface composition and topography
Cons
- Requires vacuum
- Can cause dehydration, shrinkage, or collapse of soft biological/polymeric samples
- Not suitable for living or hydrated samples  
- Water evaporates rapidly → samples must be dried or frozen

Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM)

WHAT IS AFM?

- Generates 3D surface maps
- Resolution high enough to distinguish individual atoms
- Works by physically “feeling” the surface with a sharp tip

AFM Characterization — Information Obtained
- Topographical properties  
    - Surface height
- Roughness
- Mechanical properties  
- Stiffness
- Tensile strength
Biological Relevance of AFM
- Works in liquid environments
- Enables imaging of:  
- Live cells
- Proteins in native conformations
- Membranes
- Biomolecular compounds
AFM Pros / Cons
Pros
- Non-destructive at low forces
- Suitable for delicate samples
- Extremely versatile:  
- Soft materials (hydrogels, polymers)
- Biological materials (enzymes, proteins)
- Synthetic materials (ceramics, metals)
Cons
- Limited scan size (typically microns)
- Limited depth information
- Primarily a surface technique
- Not suitable for thick, opaque samples when internal structure is needed

What / Purpose

• Surface analysis technique used to determine atomic and molecular composition

• Used for:

• Surface chemistry

• Depth profiling

• Impurity detection

• Especially valuable for biomaterials and tissue engineering interfaces

How SIMS Works (Process)

1. Sample placed in SIMS chamber

2. Primary ion beam (Cs⁺, O₂⁺, or Ga⁺) bombards surface

3. Surface atoms are sputtered and ionized

4. Secondary ions are ejected

5. Mass spectrometer separates ions by mass-to-charge ratio

6. Detector measures ion intensity

Output & Results

• Mass spectra → elements present

• Elemental maps → spatial distribution of elements

• Depth profiles → concentration vs depth  

Main Components

• Ion source

• Sample chamber

• Mass spectrometer

• Detector

• Data analysis system
Energy source: Primary ion beams (Cs⁺, O₂⁺, Ga⁺)

Relevance to Biological Performance

• Analyzes nanoscale surface chemistry contacting cells

• Critical for:

• Cell adhesion and spreading

• Detecting Ca, P, proteins at interfaces

• Scaffold–tissue integration

• Tracking degradation and elemental changes over time  

X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS)

What XPS Is

• Analytical technique to examine atomic composition and chemical states

• Surface-sensitive: analyzes top few nanometers

• Detects nearly all elements except hydrogen (and helium)  

How XPS Works

1. Surface exposed to X-rays

2. X-rays eject core electrons

3. Emitted photoelectrons collected

4. Analyzer measures kinetic energy

5. Binding energy calculated → identifies element & chemical state

What XPS Tells You

• Which elements are present

• Elemental concentrations

• Valence / bonding state

• Chemical environment (what atoms are bonded to)

Data Output

• Plot of photoelectron intensity vs binding energy

• High-resolution scans show:

• Oxidation states

• Chemical bonding differences

  XPS Limitations

• Probes only ~1–10 nm (surface only)

• Requires vacuum → not good for wet biological samples

• Cannot detect hydrogen or helium

• X-rays can:

• Break bonds

• Desorb surface groups

• Crosslink materials

• Non-conductive samples:

• Peak shifting

• Peak broadening

• Expensive and limited accessibility

  ⚠️ Key Distinction (Exam Alert)

• SIMS & XPS = characterization tools

• They analyze surfaces, they do not modify them

Surface Tailoring (Plasma-Based)

  Plasma Glow Discharge — What It Changes

• Surface energy & wettability

• Adhesion & bonding strength

• Surface roughness & morphology

• Chemical functionality

• Biocompatibility
Why Tailor Surfaces

• Improve coating/film adhesion

• Enhance cell attachment (biomedical devices)

• Increase corrosion or wear resistance

• Enable selective surface reactions without bulk damage

Plasma Etching

• Removes material using reactive plasma species

• Used for:

• Surface cleaning

• Patterning

• Roughening

• Increases surface area → improves adhesion

Plasma Deposition

• Adds thin films/coatings

• Used for:

• Protective layers

• Functional coatings

• Bioactive surfaces

• Alters surface chemistry without removing base material

  Mechanical Surface Treatment — Electric Arc Wire Spray

  What It Is

• A thermal spray coating process

• Uses two electrically charged metal wires

How It Works

1. Metallic wires fed into arc gun

2. Electric arc melts wire tips

3. Compressed air or inert gas:

• Atomizes molten metal

• Propels droplets onto substrate

4. Coating rapidly solidifies on surface

Key Outcomes

• Strongly bonded metallic coatings

• Wide range of functional properties

• Used for:

• Wear resistance

• Corrosion protection

• Surface reinforcement

  Limitations of Mechanical Surface Treatment

• Risk of surface damage

• Non-uniform roughness

• Not suitable for thin, soft, or fragile materials

• Can unintentionally alter surface chemistry

• Generates dust/debris → PPE required

• Less precise than chemical or plasma treatments
